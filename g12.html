1. Chapter 16

Cursed, cursed creator! Why did I live? Why, in that instant, did I not extinguish the spark of existence which you had so wantonly bestowed? I know not; despair had not yet taken possession of me; my feelings were those of rage and revenge...

Note: This opening demonstrates the Creature's intense existential despair and rage against Victor Frankenstein, highlighting the central theme of creator and creation. Mary Shelley, Frankenstein, 1818.

When night came I quitted my retreat and wandered in the wood; and now, no longer restrained by the fear of discovery, I gave vent to my anguish in fearful howlings...

Note: Shelley's use of natural imagery (the "cold stars" and "bare trees") contrasts the Creature's inner turmoil, reinforcing the Romantic preoccupation with nature's indifference. Critical Source: Mellor, Anne K. "Mary Shelley: Her Life, Her Fiction, Her Monsters," 1988.

The sun rose; I heard the voices of men and knew that it was impossible to return to my retreat during that day...

Note: The Creature's reflection on his "imprudence" emphasizes his capacity for moral reasoning, challenging assumptions about his monstrosity. Secondary Source: Cantor, Paul A. "Frankenstein as Modern Myth," 1976.

The pleasant sunshine and the pure air of day restored me to some degree of tranquillity; and when I considered what had passed at the cottage...

As night advanced, I placed a variety of combustibles around the cottage...

Note: The act of burning the cottage symbolizes the Creature's descent into vengeance and mirrors Victor's destructive obsession with knowledge. Mary Shelley, Frankenstein, 1818.

And now, with the world before me, whither should I bend my steps? I resolved to fly far from the scene of my misfortunes...

At this time a slight sleep relieved me from the pain of reflection, which was disturbed by the approach of a beautiful child...

Note: The encounter with the child demonstrates the Creature's conflicting desire for connection and capacity for violence when rejected. Critical Analysis: Baldick, Chris. "In Frankenstein's Shadow," 1987.

"Boy, you will never see your father again; you must come with me."

The child still struggled and loaded me with epithets which carried despair to my heart...

"This was then the reward of my benevolence! I had saved a human being from destruction, and as a recompense I now writhed under the miserable pain of a wound...

Note: The Creature's wound becomes symbolic of the emotional and physical suffering inflicted by humanity's rejection. Mary Shelley, Frankenstein, 1818.

"After some weeks my wound healed, and I continued my journey. The labours I endured were no longer to be alleviated by the bright sun or gentle breezes of spring...

But my toils now drew near a close, and in two months from this time I reached the environs of Geneva...

Note: Geneva, Victor's birthplace, serves as a geographical marker for the Creature's growing identification of Victor as the source of his misery. Historical Context: Wolfson, Susan J. "Borderlines: The Shiftings of Gender in British Romanticism," 2006.
